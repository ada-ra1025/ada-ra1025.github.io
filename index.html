Who I am.01

I'm an engineer who likes to make complicated physical systems legible.

I came up through electrical and electronics engineering, finishing with first-class honours and the award for best overall performance in my graduating cohort. That's where I learned to model systems from first principles: control loops, transfer functions, batteries, power grids. Building hardware that has to actually work is a hard lesson in respecting the physics, and it's stuck with me.

Now I'm at Imperial on an MSc fusing AI and data science with geology and geophysics, on an academic scholarship. My master's research is working toward interpretable equations for battery dynamics. Around it I've led teams to the top ML model across a 250+ cohort, trained medical imaging and ECG models from scratch on 460,789 heartbeats, and turned messy seismic, geotechnical and geospatial data into engineering decisions.

The domain keeps changing, but the method doesn't: understand the system first, then build a model you can actually interpret. I'm not interested in black boxes, and I usually take a project from the maths through the code and packaging to the documentation. I read a fair amount, and I train seriously. What I keep coming back to is systems I can take apart and understand.

What I actually do.02

Four areas I work in. The common ground is applying machine learning to real physical systems and bringing engineering intuition for how they actually behave.

/ 01

Interpretable energy & battery modelling

My research centre of gravity. I build physics-grounded, interpretable models of batteries and energy systems: equivalent-circuit and single-particle electrochemical models, reduced-order models, and symbolic-regression and neural-ODE methods that recover readable equations instead of black boxes. My MSc thesis is working toward an interpretable law for lithium-ion voltage hysteresis, recovered with differentiable physics. Alongside it I've built electrochemical simulators from scratch and optimised flow-battery sizing against real operational constraints.

PyTorch · Neural ODEs · symbolic regression (PySR) · PDE / finite-difference solvers · ECM / SPM · control & state-space
The goal is a model an engineer will trust on a battery-management chip, not just one that fits the curve.
/ 02

Applied ML & deep learning that ships

I build modern ML and ship it as real software. From-scratch deep networks (U-Nets, CNNs, LSTMs), imbalanced and cost-sensitive learning, and multimodal fusion, all packaged into installable, documented, tested pipelines. Highlights include an A* on a solo 28-hour Deep Learning challenge spanning medical imaging and ECG, and leading an 8-person team to the top flood-risk model across 250+ students.

PyTorch · scikit-learn · OpenCV · imbalanced / cost-sensitive learning · feature engineering · packaging & CI
On an 88%-imbalanced ECG set of 460,789 beats, that meant 94.4% accuracy and an 0.806 F1 on the rare abnormal beats that actually matter.

Selected work.03

01 — featuredMSc research

Discovering equations for battery hysteresis

My master's thesis. Rather than fit a black box, I use differentiable physics (a neural ODE inside a known battery model) to recover an interpretable equation for lithium-ion voltage hysteresis, then distil it with symbolic regression. The aim: the speed of a data-driven model with the readability of physics, so it can run on a battery-management chip and still be trusted.

PyTorch · Neural ODE · PySR · NumPy · differentiable RK4 solver
02Deep Learning · A*

Medical AI: imaging & ECG

A solo, 28-hour Deep Learning assessment with every network trained from scratch, graded A*. It pairs a residual U-Net that translates between MRI contrasts with a cost-sensitive bidirectional LSTM that catches rare arrhythmic heartbeats in a heavily imbalanced dataset (abnormal-beat F1 0.806).

PyTorch · U-Net · BiLSTM · masked SSIM · cost-sensitive loss

Flood-risk prediction tool

I led an 8-person team across a five-day sprint and built the risk-classification model myself: the top model across a 250+ cohort, scoring 195% above the baseline. The trick wasn't accuracy, it was optimising for the real cost of a missed flood, via custom sample weighting and an expected-score decision rule. The assessor noted "excellent output performance" and "solid software engineering practices".

scikit-learn · pandas · Random Forests · CLI · Sphinx · CI
